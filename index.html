Why Facebook?

One of the core reasons I’m so drawn to Facebook is the degree to which you empower your developers. I remember listening to a conversation on Masters of Scale between Reid Hoffman and Mark Zuckerberg where Zuckerberg discussed in detail the steps the company takes to make sure engineers aren’t suffocated in bureaucracy. When someone builds a new feature, they’re often given permission to immediately release it to a portion of Facebook’s audience. The decision whether to ultimately implement comes down to sophisticated analysis of how users interacted with the new build, but no matter the outcome, that process of gathering data is considered valuable. Anecdotally, Facebook employees have more than corroborated the process that Zuckerberg described.

That kind of ethos toward work is what drew me towards computer science in the first place. The idea that you can hack, prototype, iterate, and repeat is an amazing expression of creativity, one where you really reap the fruits of your labor. Your ventures into new technologies like Oculus are proof of a company that’s struck that magic balance between scale and constant innovation. If I had the privilege to work at Facebook, I believe I’d bring valuable skills to specific areas of your technical stack (see categories below).

AI/ML

I have a tremendous amount of respect for Facebook’s work developing and maintaining PyTorch. While I haven’t coded projects under that framework, I have built models using a very similar backdrop: Tensorflow. Libraries like these that take care of reverse mode auto-differentiation under the hood are incredible because they allow you to abstract on top of some of the more rote computations, and are inherently optimized for efficiency (through GPUs that take care of the efficient parallel processing).

My most complex work in Tensorflow was building a model for BestGig, an application that attempted to empower gig workers by providing comparative rates. I’m also enrolled in a Deep Learning where we’ll eventually build generative adversarial models, along with natural language translations. Other specifics under the umbrella of AI/ML include a smart computer opponent for the classic board game Othello, and an implementation of the ID3 algorithm that classified results with > 95% accuracy.

React

Your willingness to open source an incredible front end library like React is a great example of the engagement with the developer community that I’ve come to love from Facebook. Rather than make it a hidden technology available only internally, you decided to write clear documentation and enable thousands of teams to build beautifully smooth UIs.

I’m one of those that’s benefited, and if given the opportunity, I’d love to build on top of this incredible infrastructure. I’ve created front ends for multiple full stack applications in React, with the most notable project being a virtual fridge. Through a combination of self styled and prebuilt Material UI components, I constructed an interface that allowed users to store current ingredients, find recipes that used combinations of those ingredients, and then discover nearby grocery stores to pick up the missing elements. I also have a deeper background in traditional HTML and CSS styling, so I’m comfortable with alternatives to the JSX common to React.

API Integration

If you want to create a highly functional application, it’s important to build on the shoulders of giants. Often, those giants are APIs that allow you to seamlessly integrate a feature at scale. Facebook has been a core advocate of this approach, especially through their developer login features. One click through a login with Facebook button validates a user, and lets you track their actual actions on your site.

In a similar vein, I’ve leveraged APIs to create robust user experiences. I combined Google Maps, Places, and Spoonacular to help build a virtual fridge where users could store ingredients and then search for nearby grocery stores. I’ve used the WorldTradingData API to provide relevant financial information. And I’ve often worked on the one click login, simplifying a potentially clunky user interface.
